### Attack 1: Latency Cliff in the Agentic Hot Path

- **Attack:** The construction advertises "< 0.5 s (rapidsnark, server-side)" in §6, and "< 5 s (snarkjs, WASM)" as the browser target. An MCP-enabled agent making 10 sequential tool calls — entirely normal in a LangGraph workflow — accumulates 5 seconds of pure auth overhead with snarkjs, or 0.5 s × 10 = 5 s even with rapidsnark if the agent is multi-hop. WorkOS and Auth0 verify a JWT in microseconds (Ed25519 sig check on a cached public key). The construction's §7 deployment scenario involves a CUSO platform with 200 CUs' agents calling it — this is a high-frequency, multi-tenant, latency-sensitive path. The construction claims latency is acceptable but does not model compound latency across a tool-call chain, does not address whether rapidsnark is available in all deployment environments (it's a native binary that must be shipped and sandboxed), and does not explain why agentic operators would accept a 500× latency multiplier on auth overhead.

- **Why it works / why it fails:** The construction only benchmarks single-proof latency (§6). It never addresses the compound case. Agentic frameworks like LangChain, CrewAI, and Bedrock Agents make fine-grained tool calls at high frequency — 50–200 calls per agent session is not unusual. If each call requires a fresh proof (which the construction requires for `sessionNonce` freshness and nullifier non-reuse), the math is brutal. The construction can argue "batch the proof" or "cache across calls," but the nullifier design explicitly prevents reuse (§2, gadget 9), so each presentation requires a fresh proof. No caching path is described.

- **In-threat-model?** No. The construction's security argument is sound, but the latency cost is a deployment-layer blocker the construction does not address. It must either (a) define a proof-batching protocol where one proof covers multiple tool-call authorizations within a session window, or (b) quantify the compound latency for realistic agentic workloads and show it is within operator SLA tolerance.

### Attack 2: The Adversarial-AS Model Solves a Problem Enterprises Don't Believe They Have

- **Attack:** The construction's centerpiece claim — AS-blind presentation with adversarial-AS resilience (§3, §8 Gap 1, Gap 3) — is architecturally compelling but GTM-irrelevant to 95% of the buyer population. Enterprises don't adopt Auth0 and then worry that Auth0 will forge their agents' scopes. They chose the AS; they trust it; they pay for SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001, and pen tests on it. The adversarial-AS threat model in §3 ("A controls the authorization server — can issue arbitrary tokens, lie about scope membership") describes a scenario that procurement teams at regulated CUSOs will classify as "our vendor risk management process handles this." The §7 CUSO scenario argues that "each CU's compliance officer insists on independent credential issuance" — but the practical answer to this is a federated identity hub (Auth0 Organizations, Okta Workforce Identity with federation), not a ZK circuit. The construction never addresses why the target buyer cannot solve the multi-CU federation problem with existing enterprise SSO tooling.

- **Why it works / why it fails:** The construction's §8 is written for a cryptography-literate reader. The argument "a compromised AS cannot forge scope satisfaction proofs" is technically true but requires the buyer to (a) believe their AS might be compromised, (b) believe on-chain Merkle roots are a more trustworthy anchor than their existing AS SLA and audit logs, and (c) accept a new operational dependency on Base Sepolia / Base mainnet. For regulated institutions, adding a blockchain dependency introduces its own regulatory surface (FASB digital asset accounting, NCUA guidance on distributed ledger technology). The construction treats the on-chain Merkle root as obviously more trustworthy than an enterprise AS; a procurement officer will see it as an additional failure domain.

- **In-threat-model?** No. The construction's security argument is correct within its threat model, but the threat model itself is not validated against the stated buyer segment (regulated CUSOs). The construction must either (a) cite a documented incident where a federated AS was compromised to issue inflated agent scopes in a regulated financial context, or (b) reframe the adversarial-AS property as a compliance differentiator (e.g., cryptographic proof of scope enforcement independent of any vendor's attestation) rather than a practical threat mitigation.

### Attack 3: Blinding Factor Creates a New Catastrophic Failure Mode with No Recovery Path

- **Attack:** The construction's hardened blinded-leaf design (§2, gadget 4; §5) introduces `blindingFactor` as a per-credential private input that is "chosen at enrollment, kept secret" and "stored alongside the agent's private key material." If this value is lost, the agent cannot supply it as a private witness to the circuit and **cannot generate any proof**, even if it retains the full credential commitment, EdDSA signature, and Merkle proof siblings. The construction acknowledges this implicitly ("The `blindingFactor` is stored alongside the agent's private key material and never transmitted" — §2, gadget 4) but provides zero guidance on: backup and recovery procedures, rotation on suspected compromise, what happens during key rotation if the blinding factor and signing key are stored separately, whether the on-chain registry supports re-enrollment with a new blinding factor without exposing the old `credentialCommitment`. Auth0 and WorkOS lose a client secret — the operator rotates the secret in a UI in 60 seconds. Bolyra loses a blinding factor — the agent is permanently locked out until a new enrollment is submitted (with a new `leafCommitment`), and the old leaf is orphaned on-chain until explicitly removed.

- **Why it works / why it fails:** This is a usability and operational security attack, not a cryptographic one. The construction argues correctly that the blinding factor is required for the SP game (§3, §4 SP reduction) — removing it reverts to the broken prior construction where `credentialCommitment` is on-chain. But the operational consequence of a new secret that must be managed, backed up, and rotated in a regulated environment is non-trivial. NCUA §748 requires financial institutions to have incident response plans for credential compromise. The construction's §7 CUSO scenario involves 200 CUs, each with multiple agents, each with a `blindingFactor` that is separate from and not recoverable from the operator signing key. The construction also does not specify whether the `blindingFactor` is entropy-equivalent to the signing key (256 bits?) or a smaller field element, affecting its KMS storage requirements.

- **In-threat-model?** No. The construction does not define a `blindingFactor` lifecycle: generation entropy requirements, storage recommendations, backup procedures, rotation protocol on suspected exposure, and the re-enrollment flow when rotation is required. Without this, the blinded-leaf construction cannot be operationalized in a regulated environment, regardless of its cryptographic correctness.

### Attack 4: The 30-Root History Buffer Creates an Unquantified Revocation Window for Financial-Tier Permissions

- **Attack:** The construction specifies a "30-entry circular buffer on-chain" for root history (§5, Bolyra primitive mapping; §7 step 4: "checks `agentMerkleRoot` ∈ on-chain root history buffer (last 30 roots)"). Revocation is handled by "removing the blinded leaf" and updating the Merkle tree (§7, step 5). But the construction never specifies: (a) how frequently the on-chain Merkle root is updated, (b) how many state transitions a revoked leaf survives in the 30-root buffer before it is fully invalidated, or (c) what the maximum revocation-to-invalidation wall-clock time is. For an agent holding `FINANCIAL_UNLIMITED` (bit 4) or `SIGN_ON_BEHALF` (bit 5) — which the construction explicitly encodes and enforces via cumulative-bit constraints (§2, gadget 7) — a revocation window where a compromised agent can continue generating valid proofs is a material compliance gap. Auth0 and Stytch revoke tokens with immediate effect: they invalidate the opaque token on the introspection endpoint, and any RS that calls introspection gets `{"active": false}` within milliseconds. The construction's AS-blind design — its key differentiation in §8 Gap 1 — directly causes this revocation gap: because there is no AS roundtrip, there is no revocation propagation channel. The only revocation mechanism is Merkle tree update, which is batch and delayed.

- **Why it works / why it fails:** The construction correctly notes that nullifier-based replay detection prevents reuse of a specific `(proof, sessionNonce)` pair (§2, gadget 9). But it does not prevent the compromised agent from generating *new* proofs with fresh `sessionNonce` values against stale-but-still-valid Merkle roots in the 30-root buffer. If roots are updated every block (Base Sepolia: ~2 seconds) and each update removes the revoked leaf, the 30-root buffer represents up to 60 seconds of revocation latency in the best case. If root updates are batched less frequently (common for cost reasons), the window extends proportionally. The construction's §7 regulatory argument — that NCUA examiners can audit enrollment — does not address the NCUA's incident response expectations for credential revocation: NCUA §748 Appendix B (Incident Response Programs) and the 2022 NCUA cybersecurity guidance expect "immediate" revocation of compromised access. A 30-root buffer with unspecified update frequency does not meet this bar for `FINANCIAL_UNLIMITED` permissions.

- **In-threat-model?** No. The construction must either (a) specify a maximum root-update frequency and bound the revocation window (e.g., "roots update every 2 seconds on Base; the 30-root buffer covers at most 60 seconds; operators must consider this SLA"), or (b) define an emergency revocation mechanism that invalidates a specific `leafCommitment` across all historical roots simultaneously (e.g., an on-chain nullifier blacklist for blinded leaves). Without this, the AS-blind property — the construction's primary differentiation over RFC 7662 — directly creates a revocation gap that is incompatible with the NCUA compliance narrative in §7.

### Attack 1: Subverted Phase 2 Ceremony — SSU Reduces to a 1-of-N MPC You Did Not Specify

**Attack:**
The construction's primary proving system is Groth16 with a **project-specific Phase 2** trusted setup for `AgentPolicy` (§5, "Proving system (primary): Groth16 with project `pot16.ptau` Phase 1"). Phase 1 reuses Semaphore's public ceremony, but Phase 2 generates circuit-specific `(.zkey)` and is entirely in Bolyra's control. If any single Phase 2 participant leaks or computes the "toxic waste" `(α, β, γ, δ)`, the adversary can construct proofs for arbitrary public inputs with arbitrary private witnesses — specifically, forge a proof claiming `permissionBitmask & requiredScopeMask == requiredScopeMask` when no valid credential exists in the Merkle tree. This completely breaks **SSU** step 1 (§3, Game SSU): the on-chain verifier accepts `(π*, pubSignals*)` without any valid enrollment.

**Why it works / why it fails:**
The construction states the SSU game "Claim: No PPT adversary wins SSU with non-negligible probability under the assumptions in §4," and A1 is labeled "Knowledge soundness of Groth16 (in the generic group model + random oracle model for Fiat-Shamir)." But **knowledge soundness of Groth16 assumes an honestly generated CRS**. Under a subverted CRS, Groth16 provides zero security — the extractor breaks down entirely, and the reduction sketch in §3 falls apart at step 2 ("By A1, extract witness w..."). PLONK is offered as an alternative (§5), but the construction designates Groth16 as primary with PLONK as optional — it does not specify a fallback policy triggered by ceremony compromise.

The construction names the assumption but does not specify: (a) how many parties participate in the Phase 2 ceremony, (b) the threshold (1-of-N? majority?), (c) whether the ceremony transcript is public and verifiable, or (d) what the recovery path is if toxic waste leaks post-deployment while credentials are live. The "PLONK avoids per-circuit ceremony" note in the circuit table (§2) is the right direction but is left as an optional path, not the default.

**In-threat-model?** No — this is not addressed. The threat model says "The Groth16/PLONK trusted setup (honest-majority ceremony assumption)" is outside adversary control, but **this is an assumption, not a construction property**. The adversary does not need to break the ceremony in real time; a single malicious insider at ceremony time (possibly years before deployment) retroactively breaks every proof ever generated or to be generated. The construction must either: (i) specify a verifiable N-of-N ceremony with public transcript and provide the ceremony parameters, (ii) mandate PLONK as the primary (universal setup, no per-circuit toxic waste), or (iii) formally bound SSU under the weaker "subversion-resilient SNARK" definition from Bellare-Fuchsbauer-Scafuro 2016.

### Attack 2: AS-Knows-credentialCommitment — the SP Game's Adversary Model Is Inconsistent With the Deployment Scenario

**Attack:**
The overall threat model (§3, Adversary Capabilities) states: "The adversary A controls: The authorization server (AS)." The SP game (§3, Game SP) simultaneously asserts the adversary has access to "leafCommitment₀ and leafCommitment₁, but NOT credCommitment₀, credCommitment₁, blind₀, or blind₁."

These two statements are in tension. In the concrete deployment scenario (§7, Federated Credit Union), the operator signs `credentialCommitment = Poseidon5(modelHash, opAx, opAy, permissionBitmask, expiryTimestamp)` (§2, gadget 2). If the AS is the operator — or if the enrollment protocol routes `credentialCommitment` through the AS for any reason (to register the credential, obtain a receipt, etc.) — then a compromised AS knows `credCommitment_i` for every agent it enrolled.

Given `credCommitment_i`, the attack on SP is trivial: observe `nullifierHash = Poseidon2(credCommitment_c, sessionNonce)` as a public output. Compute `Poseidon2(credCommitment_0, sessionNonce)` and `Poseidon2(credCommitment_1, sessionNonce)`. Compare. Win SP with probability 1 — no computational assumption needed. The blinded-leaf construction provides zero protection against an adversary who already holds `credentialCommitment`, because blinding only protects the on-chain leaf (`leafCommitment = Poseidon2(credCommitment, blindingFactor)`); it does not prevent the adversary from directly computing the nullifier.

**Why it works / why it fails:**
The SP game's reduction argument (§3) explicitly says: "the adversary cannot recover `credCommitment₀` or `credCommitment₁` from these values." This conclusion is valid only if `credCommitment_i` is never exposed to the adversary through any channel. But the construction's threat model grants the adversary AS control, and the enrollment protocol does not specify that `credentialCommitment` is kept off the AS's view. The §7 scenario has "each CU operating AI agents" with the CUSO as the shared platform — it is plausible that the CUSO AS handles enrollment. Even if the AS and operator are separate, the construction does not require or enforce this separation.

The construction claims in §8 (Gap 3, Adversarial-AS): "A compromised AS cannot forge scope satisfaction proofs for agents it did not enroll." But this only addresses SSU (forgery), not SP (privacy). An AS that did enroll an agent and retained `credentialCommitment` in the process breaks SP without forging anything.

**In-threat-model?** No. The SP game as written is sound only when AS ≠ operator AND `credentialCommitment` is never transmitted outside the agent. This must be stated as an explicit protocol invariant (with enforcement mechanism), not left to deployment convention. The game definition must be revised to either: (i) restrict the AS-control assumption to SSU only and separately specify what the AS learns during enrollment, or (ii) redesign the nullifier so it does not depend on `credentialCommitment` in a way that a credential-aware AS can exploit — e.g., `nullifierHash = Poseidon2(leafCommitment, sessionNonce)`, which uses the on-chain value and gives the AS no leverage even if it knows `credentialCommitment`.

### Attack 3: Adaptive Bit-Probing by a Malicious RS — Full Bitmask Recovery in 64 Queries

**Attack:**
The SP game (§3) is defined over a **single proof presentation**. It protects "bits of `permissionBitmask` beyond the predicate outcome" in one shot. But the scope satisfaction gadget (§2, gadget 6) is a bit-by-bit evaluation: for each `i`, `reqBits[i] * (1 - permBits[i]) === 0`. If `requiredScopeMask` has exactly one bit set, the proof reveals exactly one bit of `permissionBitmask`.

A malicious RS adaptively probes with `requiredScopeMask = 2^i` for `i = 0, 1, ..., 63`. The agent, having no choice about which proofs to generate for resource access, generates 64 proofs. After 64 successful presentations (all with distinct `sessionNonce`, so nullifier replay is avoided), the RS has learned every bit of `permissionBitmask`. The session-randomized `scopeCommitment` and `nullifierHash` provide no protection against this — they prevent cross-session linkability, but the RS is the same party in all 64 queries.

**Why it works / why it fails:**
The claim in §1 says the RS "learns nothing about `permissionBitmask` beyond `permissionBitmask & requiredScopeMask == requiredScopeMask`." This is true for each individual proof. But for 64 proofs with adaptively chosen single-bit masks, the RS learns the entire bitmask as the conjunction of 64 individual predicate results. The zero-knowledge property, correctly applied, means each proof reveals exactly what the public inputs commit to revealing — and if the public inputs include a single-bit `requiredScopeMask`, that is exactly one bit.

The multi-session extension in §3 addresses cross-session correlation via `credentialCommitment` recovery, but this is a different threat. Bit-probing requires no cryptographic attack — it is a legal, specification-conformant use of the protocol. The construction in §8 says the RS "learns only that the predicate holds," which is true per-proof but misleading about the multi-proof information profile.

**In-threat-model?** Partially. The formal SP game does not cover it (single-proof). But the practical deployment scenario (§7) assumes the RS is semi-trusted and motivated adversarially ("RS needs cryptographic assurance independent of AS cooperation"). A semi-trusted RS that is also adversarial can use adaptive bit-probing to recover the full bitmask, defeating the competitive intelligence motivation in §7 ("an agent from CU-A ... must prove ... without revealing that it also holds `FINANCIAL_UNLIMITED`").

The construction must either: (i) extend SP to a multi-proof adaptive game and prove security there (it will likely fail), or (ii) acknowledge that Selective Scope Privacy holds only against a **non-adaptive** RS (fixed `requiredScopeMask`) and document this as a deployment constraint — RSes must not be permitted to vary `requiredScopeMask` across sessions with the same agent. The current framing as "runtime-adaptive" (§1) makes this worse, not better: it positions adaptive mask selection as a feature while ignoring the adversarial direction of that adaptivity.

### Attack 4: Simulation Extractability Gap — Delegation Composition Lacks a Proof

**Attack:**
The construction describes composing `AgentPolicy` with a `Delegation` circuit (§2, §5 spec divergence note 3): "The Delegation circuit's chain-linking constraint becomes `Poseidon3(delegatorScope, delegatorCredCommitment, previousSessionNonce) == previousScopeCommitment`." This composition — where a proof's public output (`scopeCommitment`) becomes a private input to a downstream circuit — requires **simulation extractability (SE)** of the underlying SNARK, not just knowledge soundness.

Groth16 is knowledge sound in the generic group model, but it is **not known to be simulation-extractable** without additional algebraic assumptions or a non-standard model. Bolyra's security argument for Delegation assumes that given a valid `(π_AgentPolicy, π_Delegation)` pair, an extractor can independently extract witnesses from each proof. But a concurrent adaptive adversary who can request AgentPolicy proofs and then compose them into Delegation proofs can defeat a non-SE extractor: the Delegation proof's validity does not imply the AgentPolicy proof was honestly generated — it only implies that the Delegation prover *knows* inputs consistent with `scopeCommitment`. If `scopeCommitment` was malformed or the AgentPolicy proof was forged via a subverted setup (see Attack 1), the chain-linking constraint does not propagate soundness.

Formally: the SSU game for the Delegation circuit must reduce to the SSU game for AgentPolicy via a sequential extractor. In the UC framework, this requires the underlying proof system to realize the `F_ZK` ideal functionality against **malicious provers and verifiers simultaneously** — which for Groth16 requires the Sasson-Tromer-Vilner "zk-SNARK in ROM" construction or equivalent. None of this is cited or argued.

**Why it works / why it fails:**
The construction's §3 reduction sketch extracts a witness from a single AgentPolicy proof and derives a contradiction from Poseidon collision resistance. This is sound for isolated AgentPolicy proofs. But for a delegated credential chain, the argument must also show that a valid `(π_AgentPolicy, π_Delegation)` pair implies both circuits were honestly executed. Without SE, an adversary can construct a `π_Delegation` that passes the chain-linking constraint for a `previousScopeCommitment` that was never produced by an honest AgentPolicy execution — the Delegation circuit only checks the arithmetic of the linking constraint, not provenance.

The PLONK alternative is better positioned here: recent results (e.g., Fuchsbauer-Kiltz-Loss 2018 via Fiat-Shamir in ROM) give simulation extractability for certain PLONK variants, but the construction does not cite these results and does not mandate PLONK for the Delegation circuit.

**In-threat-model?** No — Delegation is explicitly in scope (§2, §5) but the composition security argument is absent. The construction must either: (i) prove SE for the specific Groth16 instantiation used (this likely requires the algebraic group model + ROM together), (ii) mandate PLONK for all circuits involved in delegation chains and cite an SE theorem for the instantiation, or (iii) redraw the threat model to exclude adaptive adversaries in delegation contexts and document this as a known limitation. Until one of these is done, the claim that the Delegation circuit "enforces [scope narrowing] on-chain" (§1 and CLAUDE.md) is not backed by a reduction from any named assumption in §4.

### Attack 1: Audit Trail Opacity Under NCUA Part 748 / GLBA Safeguards Rule

- **Attack:** At 2am, a fraud alert fires on a member account. The construction explicitly celebrates that "the RS learns nothing about `permissionBitmask` beyond the predicate outcome." The NCUA examiner needs the opposite: a complete, legible audit trail mapping each access event to a named agent, a named operator, and an authorization basis they can cross-reference against the CU's vendor contracts and GLBA access logs.

You cannot identify unauthorized access from opaque field elements. If the CUSO platform's RS logs only Groth16 public signals, the incident responder cannot satisfy this requirement without offline correlation using private material that, by construction, the RS never held.

It does not address the regulatory adversary whose job is to read the audit log and hold someone accountable. The construction must specify a parallel auditor path: either (a) the RS stores a decryptable mapping from `nullifierHash` to operator identity at log time (requiring the operator to supply a linkage certificate at proof time, breaking the AS-blind claim), or (b) a trusted audit escrow holds the `(nullifierHash → credentialCommitment → operator)` mapping under legal-process disclosure rules. Neither is specified. This is a deployment-blocking gap for any NCUA-regulated entity.

### Attack 2: `blindingFactor` Key Custody — Single Point of Credential Failure

- **Attack:** Section 2 (blinded leaf commitment, gadget 4) states the `blindingFactor` is "chosen at enrollment, kept secret" and "stored alongside the agent's private key material." Section 7 states it "can be disclosed per-agent under appropriate legal process." I need to know: where specifically does it live? If it lives in an environment variable, a Kubernetes secret, or a container filesystem, that is plaintext key material outside an HSM. GLBA §314.4(c)(1) requires the institution to "implement access controls on customer information systems." A `blindingFactor` in a K8s secret does not meet FIPS 140-2 Level 2.

  Now the operational failure: the agent container crashes, the pod is rescheduled, and the new instance does not have the `blindingFactor` (it was in ephemeral memory or a lost secret). The agent's enrollment is now a dead leaf — it cannot prove Merkle membership (gadget 5 requires `blindingFactor` as private input). To recover, the CU must re-enroll: compute a new `leafCommitment` with a fresh `blindingFactor` and submit an on-chain transaction. During the window between failure and re-enrollment, the agent cannot authenticate to the CUSO platform. For a fraud-detection agent running continuously against member transaction streams, this is a production outage.

- **Why it works / why it fails:** The construction introduces the `blindingFactor` (§2, §4, §5) as a privacy hardening measure and correctly notes it closes the SP game attack (§3). But it adds a new operational secret that must be treated with the same custody discipline as a private key, without providing any key management specification. The construction mentions legal-process disclosure (§7) which implies the `blindingFactor` must be persistently retrievable — you cannot disclose it if it's ephemeral. But "persistently retrievable" and "HSM-backed" are in tension: HSMs are designed to prevent export of private key material. The construction must specify whether `blindingFactor` is exportable (auditable, but HSM-incompatible) or non-exportable (HSM-compatible, but legal-process disclosure requires the HSM to participate in a Poseidon evaluation). Neither path is trivial; neither is specified.

- **In-threat-model?** No. The adversary model in §3 assumes the honest agent's private credential fields — "including `blindingFactor`" — are uncompromised. This axiom does not survive contact with a NCUA key management audit or a 2am container failure. The construction must add a key custody specification for `blindingFactor` with at minimum: storage medium requirements, backup and recovery procedure, and the operational path for re-enrollment after credential loss.

### Attack 3: On-Chain Root Availability Is Not a Core Processor SLA

- **Attack:** Section 5 maps `agentMerkleRoot` verification to "30-entry circular buffer on-chain" (§2.1). Section 7 states the CUSO platform checks `agentMerkleRoot` against "the on-chain root history buffer." This means every API call the CUSO platform receives requires a read against Base mainnet (or Base Sepolia). Base is an OP Stack L2 with Coinbase as the sequencer. Base has had sequencer outages — the most recent significant one lasted approximately 1 hour. During such an outage, the RS cannot validate any Groth16 proof: it cannot confirm that the `agentMerkleRoot` in the public signals corresponds to a valid on-chain root. The RS has two options: (a) reject all agent requests (complete authentication outage for all 200 CUs' agents), or (b) cache roots locally and continue serving from the cache.

  Option (b) reintroduces exactly the trust model the construction claims to eliminate. If the RS caches on-chain roots, a revoked agent's root may still be in the local cache after an on-chain revocation. The construction's revocation mechanism — "removing the blinded leaf" (§7) — requires an on-chain Merkle update to produce a new root. If the RS is operating from a stale cache, revocation does not take effect until the cache is refreshed and the on-chain root is re-read. During an outage, the revocation window is unbounded.

  My core processor (Fiserv/Jack Henry/Symitar) has a 99.95%+ uptime SLA enforced by contract. I cannot accept a dependency on a public L2 sequencer with no contractual SLA for a system that gates member data access.

- **Why it works / why it fails:** The construction is technically sound in its cryptographic claims but does not address operational availability as a security property. From the FFIEC CAT perspective, "availability" is one of the five NIST Cybersecurity Framework functions. The construction's §7 regulatory value claim — "NCUA §701.27 third-party due diligence" — requires the third party (Base/Coinbase sequencer) to have documented availability controls that can be reviewed by examiners. A public blockchain is not a vendor with a SOC 2 Type II report and a vendor management agreement. NCUA examiners ask for third-party vendor contracts, business continuity plans, and incident response SLAs. The on-chain registry has none of these.

- **In-threat-model?** No. The threat model in §3 defines adversaries who attack the cryptographic construction. Infrastructure availability is not modeled. The construction must specify: (a) an RPC provider with a documented SLA (e.g., Alchemy/QuickNode enterprise with contractual uptime guarantees), (b) a root-caching policy with explicit freshness bounds and the security implication of serving from stale roots, (c) a fallback behavior when on-chain reads fail (fail-open vs. fail-closed, and the regulatory justification for each), and (d) whether the on-chain registry operator can be treated as a NCUA-regulated third party under §701.27.

### Attack 4: "Adversarial-AS" Replaces a Licensed Entity with an Unlicensed L2 Sequencer

- **Attack:** Section 8 (Gap 3: Adversarial-AS model) argues that removing the AS from the trust path is a security improvement: "the trust anchor is the on-chain Merkle root (consensus-secured)." From my vendor management desk, I read this as: you replaced a licensed OAuth 2.0 authorization server — which I can put under contract, require SOC 2 Type II from, name in my GLBA incident response plan, and hold liable — with Base L2, operated by Coinbase's sequencer under Coinbase's terms of service. Coinbase is not signing a vendor agreement with my credit union for this purpose. The "on-chain registry" has no BAA, no data processing agreement, no liability cap, no indemnification, and no NCUA-recognized third-party status.

NCUA §701.27 and the NCUA Third-Party Relationships guidance (Letter to Credit Unions 07-CU-13, 2022 update) require the CU to: identify all third parties with access to member information; assess their financial condition, reputation, and controls; and maintain written contracts. The on-chain Merkle registry is a third party with access to enrollment data (model hashes, operator public keys). I cannot satisfy §701.27 due diligence on a smart contract. I cannot obtain a SOC 2 report from Solidity bytecode.

### Attack 3: `requiredScopeMask` Is Public — Intersection Attack Reconstructs Bitmask Across Presentations

**Attack:**
The construction exposes `requiredScopeMask` as a *public input* in every proof (§2, Public Inputs table). The Scope Privacy game (§3, SP game) only protects against distinguishing two agents *that both satisfy the same predicate*. - RS1 accepts the agent's proof with `requiredScopeMask = 0x81` → agent has bits 0 and 7.
- RS2 accepts the agent's proof with `requiredScopeMask = 0x14` → agent has bits 2 and 4 (FINANCIAL_UNLIMITED).
- RS3 *rejects* the agent's proof attempt with `requiredScopeMask = 0x40` → agent does NOT have bit 6 (SUB_DELEGATE).

By contrast, the RFC 7662 baseline with per-RS AS policy NEVER reveals `requiredScopeMask` to the RS or to network observers — the AS filters the scope list before issuing a token, and the RS sees only the scopes it was given. ### Attack 4: Gap 5 (Model-Identity Binding) Is Achievable in the Baseline — Neither Stack Proves Execution

The RS verifies the proof commits to this commitment, and the commitment includes `modelHash`.

The RFC 7662 baseline equivalent: the AS issues a JWT with a custom claim `"model_hash": "<value>"`, signed by the AS's key. DPoP binds the token to an agent-specific keypair. If that keypair is derived from the model hash (e.g., `privKey = HKDF(modelHash, operatorSecret)`), then DPoP provides cryptographic binding to a model-hash-specific key. **In-threat-model?** No — Gap 5 as written is not a genuine differentiator.
