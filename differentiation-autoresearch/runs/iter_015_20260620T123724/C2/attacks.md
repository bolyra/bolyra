### Attack 1: The AS-as-Operator De-anonymization Attack

- **Attack:** In §7 (Credit Union scenario), DFCU is simultaneously the AS *and* the entity that "enrolls member agents in the Bolyra agent Merkle tree." The game's challenger-generates model decouples AS from operator; the §7 deployment couples them. The construction must either (a) explicitly exclude AS=operator deployments from the claim's scope (contradicting §7), or (b) introduce operator-blinded commitment issuance so the operator cannot reconstruct `cc` from the enrollment event.

### Attack 1: Credential Commitment Preimage Attack on `scopePseudonym`

- **Attack:** The adversary A controls the AS, which is explicitly the Merkle tree maintainer (§3: "full read access to the agent Merkle tree"). Enrollment means the AS receives and stores each leaf: `credentialCommitment = Poseidon5(modelHash, Ax, Ay, permissionBitmask, expiryTimestamp)`. The AS therefore knows *every* `credentialCommitment` in the tree — not just read access to the root, but the full leaf preimage set `{cc_1, ..., cc_N}`. Now A corrupts RS_j (permitted by the game for up to k−1 RSes), learning `scopeId_j`. The `scopePseudonym = Poseidon2(credentialCommitment, scopeId)` is a **public output** of the proof. A enumerates: for each `cc_i` in the tree, compute `Poseidon2(cc_i, scopeId_j)` and compare to the observed `scopePseudonym_j`. This is O(N) Poseidon evaluations — 400K in the deployment example. A identifies the agent with probability 1.

- **Why it works / why the reduction is broken:** Section §4 Step 1 states the PRF key is "`cc` (which is hidden as a private input)." This is false. `cc` is hidden from third parties, but the AS *built the tree* — it knows every cc. The PRF assumption A1 requires the key to be *unknown to the distinguisher*. Here the distinguisher IS the AS, and the AS knows the key. The security reduction is therefore invalid. The claimed reduction to Poseidon PRF does not go through. Formally: if you model the scope pseudonym as `f_{cc}(scopeId)`, the AS holds cc in the clear and can evaluate the PRF directly on any candidate scopeId. If instead you model it as `f_{scopeId}(cc)`, PRF security holds only when scopeId is unknown — but for corrupted RSes scopeId is revealed, reducing to the O(N) preimage search above.

- **In-threat-model?** No — the IND-UNL-AS game (§3) explicitly allows A to corrupt up to k−1 RSes. Corrupting RS_j gives scopeId_j. AS knowledge of all cc values is baked into the setup. A wins with advantage ≈ 1, not negl(λ). **The construction must address this.**

- **Fix direction:** The pseudonym requires an agent-held secret unknown to the AS: `scopePseudonym = Poseidon3(agentSecret, credentialCommitment, scopeId)` where `agentSecret` is generated at enrollment and never transmitted to the AS. This mirrors Semaphore's identity secret (trapdoor + nullifier) pattern — the identity is committed to the tree as `Poseidon2(agentSecret, credentialCommitment)`, and the circuit proves knowledge of both. The current construction omits this secret entirely, which is precisely the gap that makes the preimage attack feasible.

### Attack 2: `scopePseudonym` Is a Permanent, Non-Rotatable Within-Scope Identifier

- **Attack:** `scopePseudonym = Poseidon2(credentialCommitment, scopeId)` is deterministic and public. For a fixed (agent, RS) pair, every proof across all sessions produces an identical `scopePseudonym`. The RS accumulates a permanent, cross-session dossier: "this pseudonym has accessed our service 847 times, with the following timing distribution." There is no pseudonym epoch, no credential rotation, no mechanism to break the long-term correlation within scope. Worse, the `sessionNullifier = Poseidon3(scopePseudonym, sessionNonce, blindingScalar)` includes `scopePseudonym` as a component — and `scopePseudonym` is already public — so the sessionNullifier adds freshness but does not break the persistent within-scope identifier.

- **Why it works / why the construction does not address it:** The construction defines IND-UNL-AS only for cross-scope linkability (can the AS correlate proofs for RS_i and RS_j?). It says nothing about within-scope forward secrecy. If the RS is later compromised, or the RS is subpoenaed, or the RS colludes with the AS after the fact, the stable `scopePseudonym` retroactively links all sessions. No unlinkability game is defined for this scenario. The HIPAA deployment scenario (§7) specifically concerns long-term referral-network privacy, making this particularly relevant: if LabCorp's RS is compromised, every historical access from any agent is retroactively attributed to a stable pseudonym.

- **In-threat-model?** No — the construction is silent on within-scope session linkability and provides no forward secrecy guarantee for `scopePseudonym`. The threat model (§3) does not bound the RS's ability to track agents across sessions. **The construction must define a within-scope unlinkability game or explicitly disclaim this property.**

- **Fix direction:** Introduce a per-epoch blinding: `scopePseudonym = Poseidon3(agentSecret, scopeId, epoch)` where `epoch` ticks on a protocol-defined schedule. Alternately, use a rerandomized commitment at the cost of losing the RS's ability to recognize returning agents (which may or may not be a design goal — but the construction never states which).

### Attack 3: Groth16 Non-Simulation-Extractability in the Delegation Chain

- **Attack:** Groth16 satisfies knowledge soundness but is **not simulation-extractable (SE)**. This means given two valid Groth16 proofs `π_1`, `π_2` for (possibly different) statements, a computationally bounded adversary may produce a third valid proof `π_3` without knowing any witness, by algebraic manipulation of the group elements. The Delegation circuit (§5, Table: "Groth16 and PLONK") uses Groth16. The delegation chain links via `blindedScopeCommitment`, which §5 calls the "chain seed." The adapter constraint is a single R1CS equation: `Poseidon3(delegatorScope, delegatorCredCommitment, blindingScalar) == blindedScopeCommitment`. An adversary who observes two valid Groth16 delegation proofs — one narrowing to scope A, one narrowing to scope B — may be able to combine group elements to produce a proof that appears to narrow from A to B (or extends the chain illegitimately) without possessing a valid witness for the combined statement. No simulation-extractability analysis is provided.

- **Why it matters here:** The security of the delegation chain depends on the prover *knowing* the previous credential. Groth16 knowledge soundness establishes this only in the generic group model with a trusted setup. Non-simulation-extractability means the delegation circuit is vulnerable to **proof malleation**: an adversary can accept a delegation proof as input and output a new proof for a different (more permissive) statement. The §4 reduction sketch invokes "knowledge soundness of Groth16/PLONK (A2)" without distinguishing which proving system is used for which circuit. PLONK can be made SE; Groth16 without additional Fiat-Shamir hardening cannot.

- **In-threat-model?** Partially. The adversary model (§3) says A does not control the ZK proving process for honest provers. But delegation involves *multiple* provers across a chain, and a malicious delegatee could exploit non-SE to forge upstream capabilities. The construction does not address this. **The construction must either use SE-Groth16 / PLONK throughout the delegation circuit, or provide a malleability analysis.**

### Attack 2: JWT Introspection Response + DPoP Removes AS from the Authorization Hot Path

- **Attack:** Section 8's "structural impossibility" claim — "An AS that issues a token necessarily knows the requesting agent, the target RS, and the requested scope" — ignores `draft-ietf-oauth-jwt-introspection-response` (now published as RFC 9701). Under this extension: (1) the RS presents a token to the AS *once* and receives a signed JWT introspection response; (2) the RS caches this response for the token's lifetime; (3) subsequent resource requests from the same client are validated locally against the cached signed response — the AS is not contacted. Combine with RFC 9449 DPoP (sender-constraint binds token to the client's proof-of-possession key) and OIDC pairwise pseudonymous identifiers (PPIDs, which are per-RS and break cross-RS identity correlation at the RS level). The result: after initial issuance, the AS is not in the hot path for authorization decisions. The RS processes requests without phoning home. The AS's log contains one entry per token lifetime, not one per request. For long-lived agent credentials, the AS's visibility is comparable to Bolyra's enrollment-only AS role.

- **Why it works / why it fails:** The construction's §8 comparison table correctly notes that the initial issuance event still reveals `(agent, RS, scope, time)` to the AS. This is a real gap: RFC 9701 does not hide the *initial* token request, only subsequent resource requests. But the construction's "categorical separation" framing is overstated — it's an incremental gap at initial issuance, not a fundamental architectural impossibility. The IND-UNL-AS game in §3 should be tested against this baseline: if an RFC 9701 deployment with long-lived tokens achieves AS-blind per-request correlation, the advantage over Bolyra shrinks to "does the AS see the first handshake?" The construction has not addressed this scenario and does not quantify what fraction of real-world correlation risk is eliminated versus this baseline.

- **In-threat-model?** **Yes** — the construction survives because even with RFC 9701 caching, the AS sees the initial token exchange including the `resource` parameter (RFC 8707), which reveals the target RS. Bolyra's `scopeId` is a private circuit input visible to no one but the RS. But the construction owes an explicit comparison against the RFC 9701+DPoP+PPID combination and must narrow its claim to "AS cannot correlate *any* authorization event" rather than the current framing that implies RFC 7662 cannot match on the hot-path problem.

### Attack 3: The blindingScalar Out-of-Band Channel Is an Architectural Assumption, Not a Cryptographic Guarantee

- **Attack:** The entire privacy argument against AS visibility of the target RS rests on step 5 of the protocol: "Agent transmits `blindingScalar` to the RS over the direct TLS channel (same channel used in step 1)." The threat model in §3 asserts "A does NOT control the direct TLS channel between agent and at least one honest RS." But this is a *network topology assumption*, not a cryptographic property, and it is violated in many real deployments the construction targets. In the DFCU scenario: DFCU controls the network the member's agent runs on (home router, mobile carrier, VPN). DFCU could run TLS inspection (RFC-compliant with enterprise certificate pinning). This argument collapses if the AS operates the network. The construction should note that `blindingScalar` confidentiality requires either (a) mutual TLS with RS-controlled keys not accessible to the AS, or (b) end-to-end encryption of the `blindingScalar` under the RS's public key before the agent sends it (so even a MITM sees ciphertext). Neither is specified in the construction. Without this, an AS that operates as a TLS proxy trivially recovers `blindingScalar` for every proof and can invert all `scopeTag` values.

- **In-threat-model?** **No** — the construction must either (a) specify that `blindingScalar` is encrypted under the RS's long-term public key (e.g., ECIES/HPKE, RFC 9180) before transmission, so the AS sees only ciphertext even as a TLS MITM, or (b) restrict the threat model to exclude AS-as-network-operator and explicitly call this out as a deployment constraint. As written, the construction inherits a critical non-cryptographic assumption that breaks in enterprise and financial-institution deployments — precisely the ones §7 highlights as target scenarios.

### Attack 4: The IND-UNL-AS Game Has a Restriction That Makes It Trivially Winnable in Practice

- **Attack:** The IND-UNL-AS game definition in §3 step 4 states: "A may NOT query both agents on the same `scopeId` as the challenge scope." This restriction exists to prevent the trivial distinguisher via Merkle root + known tree. But it also means the game does not model the most realistic attack: the AS issuing credentials for *k* RSes where it operates *all k* RSes (or has contractual relationships that reveal `scopeId` for all of them). In the credit-union deployment, DFCU-as-AS may have business relationships with Target, CVS, and other merchants — receiving `scopeId` values through business agreements, not by corrupting the RS. The game allows "up to k-1 corrupted RSes" but models corruption as active compromise; a passive business-agreement disclosure of `scopeId` falls outside the corruption model. More critically: the game grants A oracle access to proofs for arbitrary `scopeId` of its choosing, but the restriction in step 4 prevents querying the challenge scope with *both* agents. In real deployments, the challenge scope IS the AS's own RS — e.g., DFCU's own member portal as RS_1. DFCU learns `scopeId_{portal}` trivially. The construction's proof in §4 does not address the case where the AS knows `scopeId` for all RSes through out-of-band means rather than active corruption.

- **Why it works / why it fails:** If the AS knows `scopeId_j` for every RS through business intelligence (not cryptographic attack), then `scopeTag_j = Poseidon2(scopeId_j, blindingScalar_j)` is the only remaining unknown per proof. From the construction's own security argument: "even if the AS maintains a complete dictionary of all RS `scopeId` values, it cannot test `Poseidon2(scopeId', blindingScalar)` without `blindingScalar`." This holds cryptographically — but it relies on the IND-UNL-AS game treating `scopeId` as something the AS only learns through RS corruption. In real deployments, `scopeId = Poseidon2(RS_domain, RS_pubkey)` — both inputs are public. DFCU can compute `scopeId_Target` itself from Target's public domain and public key without corrupting Target at all. The game should account for this: A should be able to query `scopeId` values it computes itself (which is every RS's `scopeId`) without triggering the "corruption" oracle.

- **In-threat-model?** **Yes, but underspecified** — the construction survives because even with known `scopeId`, the AS needs `blindingScalar` to invert `scopeTag`, and `blindingScalar` remains protected (modulo Attack 3). But the IND-UNL-AS game is weaker than claimed: A can enumerate all `scopeId` values without active corruption, so the collusion model in §3 understates the AS's passive capability. The game restriction in step 4 should be removed and replaced with a restriction on `blindingScalar` oracle access, not `scopeId` oracle access, to accurately reflect what the construction actually protects.

### Attack 1: The "Direct TLS Channel" Assumption Is Owned by the AS in Real Deployments

The AS maintains a lookup table `{requiredScopeMask → RS identity}` learned from any interaction with those RSes (including its own test agents). ### Attack 4: The IND-UNL-AS Game Restriction (§3, Step 4) Has an Auxiliary-Agent Loophole

- **Attack:** The IND-UNL-AS game (§3, step 4) restricts: "A may NOT query both agents on the same `scopeId` as the challenge scope." But the AS controls the Merkle tree enrollment (§3, adversary capabilities). Nothing prevents A from enrolling a *third* agent `cred₂` with an **identical** `permissionBitmask` and `expiryTimestamp` — making `credentialCommitment₂` differ only in `(modelHash, operatorPubkey)` — and querying `cred₂` against the challenge `scopeId_challenge`. A receives a valid `ScopeBlindedAgent` proof for `cred₂` at `scopeId_challenge`. The `scopePseudonym` for `cred₂` at that scope differs from `cred₀` and `cred₁` (because `credentialCommitment₂ ≠ cc₀, cc₁`). A now has a reference transcript at `scopeId_challenge` produced by the same tree under AS control. The restriction in step 4 prevents A from querying the two *challenge* agents at the challenge scope, but leaves open auxiliary agent oracle queries that let A characterize the distribution of `(scopePseudonym, scopeTag, blindedScopeCommitment)` values produced for that scope. Depending on how the simulator is constructed in the reduction, this may allow A to distinguish `cred₀` from `cred₁` at the challenge scope by elimination — ruling out the reference distribution generated by `cred₂`.

- **Why it works / fails:** The attack's feasibility depends on whether the credential commitment space is large enough that auxiliary agents cannot narrow the set. With a 400K-member tree (§7.1) and random `modelHash` values, auxiliary elimination is computationally expensive. But the formal reduction in §4 does not address this loophole — the proof sketch in step 1 argues only from PRF indistinguishability on `scopePseudonym`, not from the game restriction's tightness against auxiliary oracles. The reduction needs a hybrid argument that explicitly accounts for auxiliary agent queries to close this gap.

- **In-threat-model?** Yes, but the formal proof sketch is incomplete. The construction survives in practice (large anonymity set), but the IND-UNL-AS game as written in §3 does not explicitly close the auxiliary-agent loophole, and the §4 reduction does not cite it. A clean fix: add to step 4 "A may NOT query any agent at the challenge `scopeId`" (covering auxiliary agents, not just the two challenge credentials), and update the reduction to account for the bounded number of scope-oracle queries.
